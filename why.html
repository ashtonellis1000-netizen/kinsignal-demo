What Supervisors Tell Us
What we keep hearing in interviews

These are paraphrased from conversations with safety managers, warehouse supervisors, construction foremen, and staffing agency operators. Customer voice — not statistics.

"I didn't know the 8-hour rule."

OSHA requires employers to report workplace fatalities within 8 hours and in-patient hospitalizations, amputations, or eye losses within 24 hours. Most front-line supervisors have never been told this — and their company isn't checking the clock during a crisis.

"I couldn't find the emergency contact."

Paper binders get left at the office. HR systems require a desktop login. Supervisors end up calling the main office, then HR, then the worker's cell, then waiting. Meanwhile OSHA's clock is running and the family still doesn't know.

"We wrote the report two days later from memory."

Reports are routinely written hours or days after the event, from memory. Details get compressed, witness accounts drift, and the timeline loses precision — exactly the information you need if the case is later disputed.

"I didn't know if it was OSHA-recordable."

The line between "first aid" and "recordable" isn't always obvious. A trip to the ER doesn't automatically mean recordable. But many supervisors either over-report (wasting resources) or under-report (creating liability). There's no guidance in the moment.

"The forms were scattered everywhere."

The injury description is in one place. The contact log is in another. The witness statement is on someone's phone. When legal or HR needs the full picture six months later, nobody can reconstruct it — and the company is exposed.

"We didn't get a witness statement in time."

Witnesses move on, get transferred, or simply forget. Statements collected close to the event are far more accurate and legally defensible than those collected the next day. But nobody reminds supervisors to collect them while everyone is still on-site.

Who's Most Exposed
Industries where the documentation gap is widest

BLS injury data shows that certain industries face both higher incident rates and lower documentation quality — the worst of both worlds.

🏗️

Construction

Among the highest fatality rates in private industry (BLS). Job sites change weekly; no centralized record system. Supervisors often manage crews across multiple active sites.

📦

Warehousing & Logistics

One of the highest nonfatal injury rates in private industry (BLS). High turnover means supervisors often manage workers they barely know. Emergency contacts rarely accessible on the floor.

⚡

Field Services & Trades

Electricians, plumbers, HVAC crews. Work in isolated environments with no on-site HR support. One supervisor often covers multiple crews across a metro area simultaneously.

👷

Staffing Agencies

Temp workers placed at client sites — but the staffing agency retains OSHA responsibility. Incidents cross two organizations with no shared system. Coordination breakdown is the norm.

🏭

Light Manufacturing

High incident volume, paper-based processes. Supervisors on night shifts often have no access to HR systems or emergency contact binders stored in daytime offices.

🚛

Transportation & Fleet

Incidents happen remotely. Drivers are alone when injured. Emergency contacts may be the only lifeline — and getting them in seconds is not solved by any current system.
